  5. We use as few tools as possible. Tools just gets in the way of getting anything done. We use a spreadsheet (Excel or Google Docs depending on whether the team is colocated or dispersed) for the product backlog and record cards attached to a whiteboard using blu-tac (or, again, using Google Docs if the team is dispersed). If there's no whiteboard available we just use sticky notes, sometimes with an extra strip of tape, attached directly to the wall. Nothing fancy at all, but it doesn't get in the way and it's immediately obvious to everybody. (LinkedIn3)

  12. At a previous position we started off with a SCRUM board to get used to the process of Agile as well MS Excel spreadsheets that we customized with our own formulas tailored to fit our specific needs for our system. We used this to manage the daily scrum, story points, the retrospectives at the end of the iteration as well as the metrics and burn down charts. Using the basics (SCRUM board and Excel) allowed us to identify patterns during our iteration so we were better able to manage them. I attended a Project Management Using Agile workshop and it was mentioned to start simple and then move into a tool that works best for you. That way you are still focused on the story points and tasks within the iteration and not trying to “keep up with the software.” The method of understanding the process before we put a tool in place really helped us. AgileZen.Com is an online tool that I've heard a lot about. It is online based and has sort of a 37Signals vibe. I'd be curious to know if any of you have heard of/used this. (LinkedIn3)

  5. When we first started out, we used a simple spreadsheet template we found online to manage our product backlogs, and our sprint backlogs. The more we used Scrum, the more the spreadsheet grew in complexity to include time tracking by team member & task, sprint dashboard reports, risk logs, impediment logs, and sprint retrospective notes. As our projects became more complex, we needed to manage multiple release plans, multiple projects and multiple dispersed team members. We tried using Microsoft SharePoint to manage our spreadsheets in a distributed environment, but it too became unmanageable. Quite simply, we reached the limit of what the spreadsheets were able to do for us. Our scrummasters were spending far too much managing the spreadsheets. (See the complete review of EdgeHopper)
